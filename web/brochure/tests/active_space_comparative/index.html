Abstract

We present a controlled comparative validation of the inZOR-ND bio-adaptive discovery engine against random search for automatic CASSCF active space selection. Building on two previous studies — N₂ dissociation (6-31G, C(18,6) = 18,564 candidates) and Cr₂ single-geometry (STO-3G, C(36,8) = 30.26M candidates) — we design a significantly harder benchmark: Cr₂ with 3 simultaneous geometries (R = 1.68, 2.0, 2.5 Å), requiring the active space to converge at all bond lengths.

This multi-geometry constraint reduces the convergence rate from ~100% (single geometry) to only 8.8%, and eliminates solution degeneracy: from ~77 degenerate optima (single geometry) to exactly 1. Under these conditions, inZOR-ND finds the unique optimal active space with fitness 2064.493, while random search stagnates at 2064.437 — a gap of 0.056 Hartree (35 kcal/mol), far beyond chemical accuracy (1 kcal/mol).

2. Why a Harder Benchmark?

The single-geometry Cr₂ benchmark (Study 2) revealed a surprising result: the fitness landscape had high degeneracy — approximately 77 distinct active spaces achieved identical optimal CASSCF energy. This means that even random sampling has a reasonable chance of hitting one of these many equivalent optima.

To rigorously test whether inZOR-ND provides a genuine advantage over blind search, we needed a problem where:

  • Optimal solutions are rare — few or ideally one unique optimum
  • Convergence is difficult — most random guesses fail to even produce valid results
  • The search space remains large — no reduction in combinatorial complexity

Adding 2 more Cr₂ geometries (R = 2.0 Å, R = 2.5 Å) alongside the original R = 1.68 Å achieves all three goals simultaneously. The active space must now capture correct physics at equilibrium, stretched, and near-dissociation bond lengths — a much more selective constraint.

8. New Result — Multi-Geometry PEC Benchmark (N₂)

The previous sections demonstrated that inZOR-ND outperforms random search when the fitness landscape has rare optima (Cr₂ hard). However, a different and arguably more practical question remains:

Can a method discover a single shared active space that remains valid across an entire dissociation curve?

In practical multireference quantum chemistry, selecting an active space at one geometry does not guarantee that the same active space will work at different geometries along a reaction coordinate. A CAS optimized near equilibrium may fail entirely in the stretched or dissociation regime, where multireference character becomes strongest and the correct orbital set can change qualitatively.

This benchmark tests exactly that: not "find a good CAS here," but "find a CAS that survives changing geometry."

A critical question for practical use: does a CAS optimized at one geometry transfer to other geometries? To test this, we optimized a CAS using inZOR-ND at R=1.10 Å alone (30 steps), then evaluated that same CAS at all 7 PEC geometries.

The two orbital sets differ in only 2 of 6 orbitals: the single-geometry CAS includes MOs 2 and 15 while the multi-geometry CAS includes MOs 3 and 13. This small orbital difference is enough to cause complete CASSCF failure at R=1.80 Å — evidence that local CAS optimality does not guarantee transferability.

Key finding: A 2-orbital difference in a 6-orbital active space is sufficient to cause complete CASSCF failure at stretched geometries. Single-geometry optimization is fundamentally insufficient for practical potential energy curve calculations.

11. What This Benchmark Shows

The important result is not simply that inZOR-ND achieves lower energy — it is that inZOR-ND discovers a shared active space that remains stable over the full dissociation curve, from compressed (0.90 Å) through equilibrium (1.10 Å) to complete dissociation (2.50 Å).

  • Single-geometry optimization is insufficient for practical PEC work — the transfer test proves this directly
  • Chemistry-guided heuristics (NOON, AVAS) are not optimized for multi-geometry robustness — they select orbitals based on a single reference state and do not account for how the orbital character changes along the reaction coordinate
  • inZOR-ND directly optimizes the shared CAS against the full PEC objective — this is the key methodological distinction
  • The advantage increases in the stretched regime, where multireference character becomes strongest and correct orbital selection matters most

11.1 Why This Matters Relative to Standard Heuristics

NOON and AVAS are useful and widely applied chemistry-guided heuristics. They provide reasonable starting points for active space selection in many cases. However, they are fundamentally single-reference heuristics — they derive their orbital selection from one reference wavefunction (typically HF or MP2 at one geometry) and do not explicitly optimize for multi-geometry robustness.

inZOR-ND, by contrast, directly evaluates candidate active spaces across all target geometries and selects the one that performs best everywhere simultaneously. This is a qualitatively different optimization objective, and the results show it matters: 75–169 kcal/mol improvement over NOON, growing with bond stretching.

11.2 Honest Assessment — N₂ as a Controlled Benchmark

Note: N₂ / 6-31G remains a relatively small system (18,564 candidates). Random search with the same evaluation budget can also find the optimal active space in this case, confirming that the fitness landscape has sufficient degeneracy for blind sampling to succeed. N₂ therefore serves primarily as a controlled proof-of-correctness benchmark — it verifies that inZOR-ND finds the right answer and demonstrates its advantage over chemistry heuristics. The real differentiation of inZOR-ND over random search is expected — and has been demonstrated — on larger and more ambiguous systems such as Cr₂ with multiple geometries (see Sections 4–6).
